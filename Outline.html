The methodology is explained as a two-pronged approach: a comprehensive desk review of existing data and policies, supplemented by primary data collection through interviews and focus groups with key local stakeholders.

2. State of Air Quality in Indonesia

Content Covered in this Section: This section provides a comprehensive overview of Indonesia's air pollution crisis. We will cover:

  • Pollution Levels & Trends: Analysis of key pollutants (PM2.5, PM10, NOx, SOx) and their concentrations in major urban and industrial zones, with a comparative analysis against national (NAAQS) and WHO guidelines.
  • Geographical Hotspots & Vulnerable Populations: Identification and characterization of the areas and demographic groups (by age, health, socio-economic status) that are most severely affected by air pollution.
  • Monitoring Capacity & Gaps: An inventory and evaluation of existing monitoring networks (government, private, research), assessing their geographic coverage, the range of pollutants monitored, and data accessibility. We will pinpoint key institutional and technical gaps.

Key Insight: The Scale of the Challenge

Jakarta's annual average PM2.5 concentration is 39.6 µg/m³. Compare this level to key health standards below.

3. Impacts of Air Pollution

Content Covered in this Section: Here, we quantify the severe and multi-faceted consequences of poor air quality on the nation. The analysis includes:

  • Public Health Impacts: Estimation of morbidity and mortality, the association with specific diseases (respiratory, cardiovascular), the disproportionate effects on vulnerable groups, and the burden on healthcare systems.
  • Economic Impacts: Assessment of macroeconomic costs (GDP loss) and microeconomic costs (healthcare expenditures, lost labor productivity, impacts on tourism).
  • Environmental, Climate, and Social Co-impacts: Analysis of the role of Short-Lived Climate Pollutants (SLCPs) and the influence of air pollution on quality of life and social equity.

4. Major Sources of Air Pollution

Content Covered in this Section: This section provides a granular look at the origins of air pollution in Indonesia. The report will cover:

  • Source Apportionment Insights: A review of existing source apportionment studies to assess the relative contributions of different sectors, alongside an identification of critical data gaps.
  • National Emission Inventory: An assessment of the availability and comprehensiveness of the national emission inventory and the official MOEF guidelines for its compilation.
  • Detailed Sectoral Analysis: A deep dive into key emitting sectors, including Transportation (road, maritime, fuel quality), Industry (manufacturing, SMEs), Energy (power generation), Residential (household fuel use), Waste Management (open burning), and Agriculture.

Key Insight: A Multi-Sectoral Problem

Data from Greater Jakarta reveals that while transportation is a major contributor, sectors like industry and power generation are dominant sources for specific, highly harmful pollutants like SO2.

5. The Governance and Enforcement Gap

Content Covered in this Section: This section critically examines the systems and structures intended to manage air quality. The analysis includes:

  • Institutional Arrangements: Mapping of key government agencies at national and sub-national levels, their mandates, functions, and coordination mechanisms.
  • Legal and Regulatory Landscape: A thorough review of national and sub-national laws, regulations, and standards, including NAAQS, emission standards, and fuel quality standards, highlighting issues of weak enforcement.
  • Existing Government Strategies: Analysis of national development plans (RPJMN), specific clean air action plans, and government budget allocations for air quality management.
  • Political Economy Context: The influence of political dynamics, economic priorities, and vested interests on policy formulation and implementation.

Key Insight: The Governance Gridlock

"A lack of top-down coordination creates a domino effect, paralyzing on-the-ground action"

🏢 Fragmented and Uncoordinated Governance

Indonesia's air quality governance is characterized by overlapping mandates across key ministries (MoE, MEMR, MoI) and between national/provincial authorities. Without a central, empowered body to ensure alignment, these parallel initiatives often fail to converge, leading to policy gaps and diluted impact.

Policy Fails in Practice

Even when good regulations exist, they are rendered ineffective by a systemic lack of enforcement and accountability.

Ineffective Fuel Standards: The Euro IV standard is undermined by continued subsidies for cheaper Euro II fuel.
Weak Emission Testing: Testing is often a formality with low compliance rates and minimal penalties.
Limited Industrial Oversight: PROPER ratings are largely voluntary, and CEMS is rarely implemented.

A Weak Business Case for Clean Air

The private sector lacks both pressure and incentives to invest in cleaner technologies, as polluters face little to no consequences.

High Upfront Costs: The capital expenditure (CAPEX) for any clean technology is a significant barrier for businesses.
Weak Emission Testing: Without strong financial penalties, polluting remains economically risk-free.
Limited Industrial Oversight: Unclear policies and the absence of a clear long-term roadmap increase perceived risk.

6 & 7. Stakeholder Landscape & Gap Analysis

Content Covered in this Section: This part of the report synthesizes our findings to map the path forward. We will present:

  • Stakeholder Mapping: A detailed identification of key actors across government, the private sector, civil society, academia, and international partners, assessing their current involvement and capacity.
  • Inventory of Interventions & Lessons Learned: A review of past and ongoing air pollution mitigation projects to identify successful approaches, barriers, and best practices.
  • Comprehensive Gap Analysis: A systematic analysis of what is missing, structured around Data & Knowledge, Policy & Regulation, Institutional Capacity, Finance & Investment, Public Demand, and Technology Gaps.

Key Insight: Role of Policymakers in Handling Air Pollution

Air pollution is a cross-sectoral issue requiring integrated coordination, ideally supported by a Presidential Regulation to align ministries and regional governments.

8. Opportunities & CAF's Strategic Niche

Content Covered in this Section: The final analytical section identifies concrete pathways for action. We will cover:

  • SWOT Analysis: A consolidated assessment of Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats for improving air quality in Indonesia.
  • High-Impact Intervention Areas: Prioritization of sectors and pollutant sources that offer the greatest potential for emission reductions and co-benefits (health, climate, economic).
  • Potential Strategic Niches for CAF: Identifying areas where CAF's global expertise, funding model, and convening power can add unique, catalytic value aligned with our Data, Demand, and Drive pillars.
  • Innovative Financing Mechanisms: An assessment of the potential for blended finance, public-private partnerships, and green bonds to fund clean air initiatives.

Key Insight: Where to Start for Maximum Impact

The Marginal Abatement Cost Curve (MACC) shows that improving waste management and funding industrial pollution controls are the most cost-effective "quick wins" to begin reducing PM2.5 emissions.

Alignment with Global & Portfolio Strategies

The Indonesia strategy is not developed in a vacuum. It directly contributes to CAF's global ambition by learning from and adapting the proven frameworks used in our other lighthouse portfolios.

A Cohesive Global Approach: Adapting the Theory of Change

🇬🇧 UK Strategy

Focuses on overcoming political inertia and weak targets by building an undeniable evidence base.

Data: Strengthen the economic case for action to influence policy.

Demand: Support coordinated campaigns (e.g., Healthy Air Coalition) to maintain public pressure.

Drive: Use inside-track influencing to shape the national Air Quality Strategy and target key sectors like transport and domestic heating.

🇿🇦 South Africa Strategy

Targets industrial pollution and environmental injustice in Priority Areas, linking clean air to the Just Energy Transition.

Data: Improve evidence through low-cost sensor deployment in under-monitored areas and develop localized health studies.

Demand: Empower community-based organizations in under-served areas to advocate for their right to clean air.

Drive: Support government to implement and enforce Priority Area regulations and promote corporate accountability.

🇮🇩 Proposed Indonesia Strategy

Aims to tackle fragmented governance and data gaps, focusing on Greater Jakarta as a starting point.

Data: Expand monitoring capacity in priority cities, develop early warning systems in Jakarta to reduce exposure, and support a national emission inventory.

Demand: Build public awareness of health risks and empower CSOs to advocate for stronger policies and enforcement.

Drive: Provide technical assistance to establish a coordinated Airshed Task Force and de-risk private investment in clean technologies for key sectors.

Potential Impact: Reduced PM2.5 emissions from key sectors (transport, industry) and reduced exposure for vulnerable populations in urban hotspots.
